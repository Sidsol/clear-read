# ClearRead — Fallacy & Technique Definitions

This document defines the 41 fallacy and propaganda technique categories used by ClearRead's analysis engine.

## Logical Fallacies

### 1. Straw Man
**Definition:** Misrepresenting someone's argument to make it easier to attack.

**Soft-language template:** *"This passage appears to simplify or misrepresent an opposing position. Consider checking the original source to see the full argument."*

---

### 2. Begging the Question
**Definition:** Assuming the conclusion within the premise.

**Soft-language template:** *"The reasoning here may assume the very thing it's trying to prove. Look for independent evidence that supports this claim."*

---

### 3. Ad Hominem
**Definition:** Attacking the person rather than the argument.

**Soft-language template:** *"This passage focuses on the person rather than their argument. Consider whether the underlying claim has merit regardless of who said it."*

---

### 4. Post Hoc (False Cause)
**Definition:** Assuming causation from correlation or sequence.

**Soft-language template:** *"This suggests one event caused another, but the connection may not be established. Consider whether other factors could explain this."*

---

### 5. Loaded Question
**Definition:** A question that presupposes something unproven.

**Soft-language template:** *"This question may contain an assumption that hasn't been established. Consider whether you agree with the premise before engaging with the question."*

---

### 6. False Dichotomy
**Definition:** Presenting only two options when more exist.

**Soft-language template:** *"This presents a limited set of choices. Consider whether there are other options or middle-ground positions not mentioned."*

---

### 7. Equivocation
**Definition:** Using a word with multiple meanings ambiguously.

**Soft-language template:** *"A key term here may be used in more than one sense, which could make the argument seem stronger than it is. Consider how the term is defined."*

---

### 8. Appeal to Authority
**Definition:** Using an authority figure's opinion as proof.

**Soft-language template:** *"This relies on someone's authority or credentials rather than evidence. Consider whether the cited authority is relevant to this specific topic."*

---

### 9. Hasty Generalization
**Definition:** Drawing broad conclusions from limited examples.

**Soft-language template:** *"This draws a broad conclusion from a limited number of examples. Consider whether the evidence is representative of the larger picture."*

---

### 10. Appeal to Popular Opinion
**Definition:** Arguing something is true because many believe it.

**Soft-language template:** *"This suggests something is true because it's widely believed. Popular opinion doesn't always align with evidence — consider looking at the underlying data."*

---

### 11. Slippery Slope
**Definition:** Arguing that one event will inevitably lead to extreme consequences.

**Soft-language template:** *"This suggests a chain of consequences that may not be inevitable. Consider whether each step in the chain is actually likely."*

---

### 12. Red Herring
**Definition:** Introducing an irrelevant topic to divert attention.

**Soft-language template:** *"This may introduce a topic that diverts from the main issue. Consider whether this point is directly relevant to the original discussion."*

---

### 13. False Analogy
**Definition:** Comparing two things that share superficial similarities but differ in crucial ways relevant to the argument.

**Soft-language template:** *"This comparison may not hold up under closer examination. Consider whether the things being compared are truly similar in the ways that matter."*

---

### 14. Appeal to Ignorance
**Definition:** Arguing that something is true because it hasn't been proven false, or false because it hasn't been proven true.

**Soft-language template:** *"The absence of proof against a claim does not prove it is true. Consider whether positive evidence has been provided to support this assertion."*

---

### 15. Appeal to Nature
**Definition:** Arguing that something is good because it is "natural" or bad because it is "unnatural."

**Soft-language template:** *"Something being natural does not automatically make it beneficial or desirable. Consider evaluating this on its own merits rather than its naturalness."*

---

### 16. Appeal to Tradition
**Definition:** Arguing that something is correct or better because it has always been done that way.

**Soft-language template:** *"Long-standing practice does not necessarily mean something is correct or optimal. Consider whether circumstances have changed since this tradition was established."*

---

### 17. No True Scotsman
**Definition:** Dismissing a counterexample to a universal claim by retroactively redefining the criteria to exclude it.

**Soft-language template:** *"The criteria here may be shifting to exclude inconvenient counterexamples. Consider whether the definition is being changed after the fact to protect a generalization."*

---

### 18. Guilt by Association
**Definition:** Discrediting a person or argument by linking them to something viewed negatively, without addressing the actual argument.

**Soft-language template:** *"This may attempt to discredit through association rather than addressing arguments directly. Consider whether the linkage is relevant to the actual claim being made."*

---

### 19. Cherry Picking
**Definition:** Selectively presenting only evidence that supports one's conclusion while omitting contradictory evidence.

**Soft-language template:** *"This may present only one side of the evidence. Consider looking for contradictory data or perspectives that may have been omitted."*

---

### 20. Genetic Fallacy
**Definition:** Judging something as good or bad based on its origin rather than its current merits.

**Soft-language template:** *"The origin of an idea does not determine its validity. Consider evaluating this claim on its own merits rather than who proposed it or where it came from."*

---

### 21. Composition / Division
**Definition:** Assuming what is true of a part must be true of the whole (composition), or what is true of the whole must be true of each part (division).

**Soft-language template:** *"What is true of a part may not be true of the whole, and vice versa. Consider whether this generalization from part to whole (or whole to part) is justified."*

---

### 22. Anecdotal Evidence
**Definition:** Relying on personal stories or isolated examples as definitive proof rather than systematic evidence.

**Soft-language template:** *"This relies on a personal story or isolated example rather than systematic evidence. Consider whether broader data might tell a different story."*

---

### 23. Appeal to Consequence
**Definition:** Arguing that a belief is true or false based on whether its consequences would be desirable or undesirable.

**Soft-language template:** *"Whether consequences are desirable or undesirable does not determine whether something is actually true. Consider the evidence independently of the implications."*

---

### 24. Shifting Burden of Proof
**Definition:** Placing the responsibility of proof on the wrong party — demanding that skeptics disprove a claim rather than requiring the claimant to prove it.

**Soft-language template:** *"The responsibility to prove a claim typically lies with the person making it. Consider whether evidence has been provided, or if you are being asked to disprove an unsupported assertion."*

---

## Propaganda & Manipulative Rhetoric

### 25. Appeal to Emotion
**Definition:** Using emotional manipulation (fear, pity, anger, pride, guilt) instead of logical argument to persuade.

**Soft-language template:** *"This passage may rely on emotional appeal rather than evidence. Consider whether the underlying claim stands on its own merits."*

---

### 26. Whataboutism
**Definition:** Deflecting criticism by pointing to someone else's behavior. Also called tu quoque.

**Soft-language template:** *"This may deflect from the issue by pointing to another party's actions. Consider whether the original concern is still valid regardless."*

---

### 27. Loaded Language
**Definition:** Using emotionally charged words to influence perception beyond what the facts support.

**Soft-language template:** *"The language here may be chosen to evoke a strong emotional response. Consider how more neutral phrasing might change your perception."*

---

### 28. False Equivalence
**Definition:** Treating two significantly different things as if they are essentially the same.

**Soft-language template:** *"This may equate two situations that differ in important ways. Consider whether the comparison is fair given the differences."*

---

### 29. Circular Reasoning
**Definition:** Using the conclusion as a premise in the argument. The claim is "proven" by restating it in different words.

**Soft-language template:** *"The reasoning here may be circular — the conclusion may be restated as evidence. Look for independent support for this claim."*

---

### 30. Glittering Generalities
**Definition:** Using vague, emotionally appealing "virtue words" that sound noble but carry no specific, verifiable meaning.

**Soft-language template:** *"This uses broad, positive-sounding language that may lack specific, verifiable meaning. Consider asking what concrete actions or evidence support these appealing-sounding words."*

---

### 31. Plain Folks
**Definition:** Attempting to convince the audience that a speaker or idea is "of the people" by portraying themselves as ordinary individuals.

**Soft-language template:** *"This may attempt to build trust through manufactured relatability rather than evidence. Consider whether the 'common person' appeal is a substitute for substantive argument."*

---

### 32. Transfer
**Definition:** Associating an idea or person with something respected (or hated) to transfer that prestige (or stigma) onto them.

**Soft-language template:** *"This may use respected symbols or institutions to lend credibility. Consider whether the association is genuinely relevant or is being used to borrow unearned authority."*

---

### 33. Testimonial
**Definition:** Using endorsements from public figures or celebrities to promote a position, especially when they lack relevant expertise.

**Soft-language template:** *"This uses a well-known figure's endorsement to build credibility. Consider whether the endorser has relevant expertise on this specific topic."*

---

### 34. Name Calling / Labeling
**Definition:** Attaching a negative label or derogatory term to a person, group, or idea to create prejudice without examining evidence.

**Soft-language template:** *"This may use negative labels to create prejudice rather than engaging with the actual argument. Consider whether the characterization is fair and evidence-based."*

---

### 35. Scapegoating
**Definition:** Blaming a specific person or group for complex problems they did not cause, diverting attention from actual causes.

**Soft-language template:** *"This may oversimplify blame for a complex problem by directing it at a specific group. Consider whether the actual causes may be more systemic or multifaceted."*

---

### 36. Fear Mongering
**Definition:** Deliberately exaggerating threats or dangers to create panic and suppress rational analysis.

**Soft-language template:** *"This may exaggerate a threat to create urgency or fear. Consider whether the danger described is supported by evidence and proportionate to the response being advocated."*

---

### 37. Dog Whistle
**Definition:** Using coded language that appears innocent to a general audience but carries specific meaning to a targeted subgroup.

**Soft-language template:** *"This language may carry coded meaning beyond its surface interpretation. Consider whether certain phrases might signal something specific to a targeted audience."*

---

### 38. Innuendo
**Definition:** Implying something negative without explicitly stating it, allowing plausible deniability.

**Soft-language template:** *"This may imply something negative without stating it directly. Consider whether the suggestion is supported by evidence or is relying on implication alone."*

---

### 39. Projection
**Definition:** Accusing opponents of the very behavior or tactics the accuser is guilty of.

**Soft-language template:** *"This accusation may mirror the accuser's own conduct. Consider whether the criticism being leveled could also apply to the person making it."*

---

### 40. Sloganeering
**Definition:** Using catchy, memorable phrases or slogans as a substitute for substantive argument.

**Soft-language template:** *"This relies on a catchy phrase rather than substantive reasoning. Consider whether a memorable slogan is being used as a substitute for actual evidence."*

---

### 41. Euphemism / Dysphemism
**Definition:** Strategically replacing accurate language with softer terms (euphemism) or harsher terms (dysphemism) to manipulate perception.

**Soft-language template:** *"The language here may be chosen to make something sound better or worse than it actually is. Consider what more neutral or precise terminology might look like."*
